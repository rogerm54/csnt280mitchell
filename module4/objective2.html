Objective 4.2 - Selecting data from a relational database

Selecting all fields and all records from a table

The easiest select query to set up is one that selects all fields and all records from a table. Using the data from our bookstore_db database that was initialized in the last lessong , here are some examples: 


bookstore_db=> select * from books;
id  |       title        |      author      | price 
----+--------------------+------------------+-------
  1 | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
  2 | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
  3 | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99

bookstore_db=> select * from customers;
id  | first_name | last_name 
----+------------+-----------
  1 | Jane       | Doe
  2 | John       | Doe
  3 | Bill       | Gates

bookstore_db=> select * from sales_view;
first_name | last_name |       title        |      author      | price 
-----------+-----------+--------------------+------------------+-------
Jane       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Jane       | Doe       | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
John       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
John       | Doe       | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99
Bill       | Gates     | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Bill       | Gates     | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
Bill       | Gates     | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99

When you use the asterisk (*) for the field names, all the fields are returned. As you can see, the basic syntax for a simple SQL SELECT looks like this: 


select <fields> from <tablename>;

Specifying fields while selecting all records from a table

Suppose you don't want to show all the fields from a table or view, you just specify the fields separated by a comma. Here is an example selecting fields from the sales_view: 


bookstore_db=> select first_name,last_name,title from sales_view;
first_name | last_name |       title        
-----------+-----------+--------------------
Jane       | Doe       | Go Dog Go
Jane       | Doe       | The Cat in the Hat
John       | Doe       | Go Dog Go
John       | Doe       | Ender's Game
Bill       | Gates     | Go Dog Go
Bill       | Gates     | The Cat in the Hat
Bill       | Gates     | Ender's Game

You can also specify fields in a different order as well as shown next: 


bookstore_db=> select title,author,first_name,last_name from sales_view;
      title        |      author      | first_name | last_name 
-------------------+------------------+------------+-----------
Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | Jane       | Doe
The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | Jane       | Doe
Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | John       | Doe
Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | John       | Doe
Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | Bill       | Gates
The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | Bill       | Gates
Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | Bill       | Gates

Suppose you only want to return some of the records from a table or view based on some criteria. This is accomplished using the where clause discussed next.

The Where Clause

Let's look at how the where clause can be used to filter the select query results. The where clause is used to state a test condition that must be matched for the select query. Consider the following query: 


bookstore_db=> select * from sales_view where last_name='Doe';
first_name | last_name |       title        |      author      | price 
-----------+-----------+--------------------+------------------+-------
Jane       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Jane       | Doe       | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
John       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
John       | Doe       | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99

For this select query, the test condition that must be matched is: 


last_name='Doe'

As you can see, the query only returns sales that have customers with a last name of Doe.

The test condition can consist of combinations of boolean expressions. Here are some examples of such combinations: 


bookstore_db=> select * from sales_view where last_name='Doe' and price>8;
first_name | last_name |       title        |      author      | price 
-----------+-----------+--------------------+------------------+-------
Jane       | Doe       | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
John       | Doe       | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99

bookstore_db=> select * from sales_view where author='Dr. Seuss' or author='P.D. Eastman';
first_name | last_name |       title        |    author    | price 
-----------+-----------+--------------------+--------------+-------
Jane       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman | 7.99
Jane       | Doe       | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss    | 8.99
John       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman | 7.99
Bill       | Gates     | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman | 7.99
Bill       | Gates     | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss    | 8.99

As you can see, the logical operators are just and and or . In the first query, the only records that are returned are those that meet the condition that last_name='Doe' and price>8 are both true. In the second query the records returned are those that match either author='Dr. Seuss' or author='P.D. Eastman'.

You can also use operators like != , >= , <= to form the test conditions for a where clause. Here are some examples of using these other operators: 


bookstore_db=> select * from books where author!='Dr. Seuss';
 id |    title     |      author      | price 
-- -+--------------+------------------+-------
  1 | Go Dog Go    | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
  3 | Ender's Game | Orson Scott Card | 9.99

bookstore_db=> select * from books where price >= 7.99;
 id |       title        |      author      | price 
----+--------------------+------------------+-------
  1 | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
  2 | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
  3 | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99

bookstore_db=> select * from books where price >= 7.99 and price < 9;
 id |       title        |    author    | price 
----+--------------------+--------------+-------
  1 | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman | 7.99
  2 | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss    | 8.99

Like and wildcards - inexact matches

The like operator is used with wildcards to create test conditions that involve inexact maches. Consider a database that returns the following results for a select query: 


carlot_db=> select * from sales_view;
 car_id | make   | model  | price    | cust_id | first_name | last_name | sex | sid 
--------+--------+--------+----------+---------+------------+-----------+-----+-----
      9 | Nissan | Maxima | 33495.00 |       1 | Jane       | Doe       | F   |   1
      4 | Honda  | Accord | 24460.00 |       2 | John       | Doe       | M   |   2
      2 | Toyota | Camry  | 23495.00 |       3 | Katherine  | Johnson   | F   |   3
      9 | Nissan | Maxima | 33495.00 |       4 | Eric       | Raymond   | M   |   4
      4 | Honda  | Accord | 24460.00 |       5 | Bill       | Gates     | M   |   5
      6 | Honda  | Fit    | 17080.00 |       6 | Ada        | Lovelace  | F   |   6
      5 | Honda  | Civic  | 19730.00 |       7 | Grace      | Hopper    | F   |   7
      4 | Honda  | Accord | 24460.00 |       8 | Scott      | Berkun    | M   |   8

The like operator can be combined with the wildcard for any sequence of characters, % . 


carlot_db=> select make,model,price,first_name,last_name,sex from sales_view where first_name like '%e';
  make | model  | price    | first_name | last_name | sex 
-------+--------+----------+------------+-----------+-----
Nissan | Maxima | 33495.00 | Jane       | Doe       | F
Toyota | Camry  | 23495.00 | Katherine  | Johnson   | F
Honda  | Civic  | 19730.00 | Grace      | Hopper    | F

The '%' pattern will match any sequence of characters for a string. So '%e' will match any first_name that ends with the letter 'e' .

You can also use the '_' pattern which matches any single character. Here is a query that uses the '_' pattern. 


carlot_db=> select make,model,price,first_name,last_name from sales_view where last_name like '_o%';
  make | model  | price    | first_name | last_name 
-------+--------+----------+------------+-----------
Nissan | Maxima | 33495.00 | Jane       | Doe
Honda  | Accord | 24460.00 | John       | Doe
Toyota | Camry  | 23495.00 | Katherine  | Johnson
Honda  | Fit    | 17080.00 | Ada        | Lovelace
Honda  | Civic  | 19730.00 | Grace      | Hopper

The '_o%' pattern will match any last_name that has a second letter that is 'o' . As you can see, all the returned rows have a last_name with the second letter being an 'o' . You can use more than one '_' as part of the matching pattern. 


carlot_db=> select make,model,price,first_name,last_name from sales_view where last_name like '___e%';
make  | model  | price    | first_name | last_name 
------+--------+----------+------------+-----------
Honda | Accord | 24460.00 | Bill       | Gates
Honda | Fit    | 17080.00 | Ada        | Lovelace

Here the matching pattern for the last_name is:

'___e%'

That three underscores at the beginning of the pattern mean that any last_name that has an 'e' for the fourth letter will match.

The where clause filters the records that are returned. To sort the returned records, you use the order by clause. The order by clause is the next subject.

The Order By Clause

Besides filtering the results of a query using the where clause, you can also sort the results on a field or several fields using the order by clause. The order by clause must always come after the where clause if a where clause is used.

Sorting on one field

The simplest cause of using the order by clause is to sort on one field. Consider the following unsorted query: 


bookstore_db=> select * from books;
 id |       title        |      author      | price 
----+--------------------+------------------+-------
  1 | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
  2 | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
  3 | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99

Let's modify this query by sorting on the author field: 


bookstore_db=> select * from books order by author;
 id |       title        |      author      | price 
----+--------------------+------------------+-------
  2 | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
  3 | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99
  1 | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99

Note how the records are sorted by the author field in alphabetical order. The default sort order is ascending. If you want to sort in descending order, you follow the field name with the key word desc . This is shown next: 


bookstore_db=> select * from books order by author desc;
id |       title         |      author      | price 
----+--------------------+------------------+-------
  1 | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
  3 | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99
  2 | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99

Notice how the records are sorted by author, but in descending order. As another example, let's sort the books table by price in descending order. This is shown next: 


bookstore_db=> select * from books order by price desc;
 id |       title        |      author      | price 
----+--------------------+------------------+-------
  3 | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99
  2 | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
  1 | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99

Sorting by more than one field

It is possible to sort by more than one field. You separate the fields by commas with the first field being the primary sort key and the second field being the secondary sort key. Consider first this unsorted query: 


bookstore_db=> select * from sales_view;
first_name | last_name |       title        |      author      | price 
-----------+-----------+--------------------+------------------+-------
Jane       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Jane       | Doe       | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
John       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
John       | Doe       | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99
Bill       | Gates     | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Bill       | Gates     | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
Bill       | Gates     | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99

Now, let's sort this by last_name , then by title : 


bookstore_db=> select * from sales_view order by last_name,title;
first_name | last_name |       title        |      author      | price 
-----------+-----------+--------------------+------------------+-------
John       | Doe       | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99
John       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Jane       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Jane       | Doe       | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
Bill       | Gates     | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99
Bill       | Gates     | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Bill       | Gates     | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99

Note that this is primarily sorted by the last_name field. Then for each customer, the records are sorted by the title of the book. For example, look at Bill Gates' purchases. The books he bought are listed in alphabetical order by title. Notice that John Doe shows up before Jane Doe. If you wanted Jane to show up before John, we would need to include first_name as one of the secondary sort fields: 


bookstore_db=> select * from sales_view order by last_name,first_name,title;
first_name | last_name |       title        |      author      | price 
-----------+-----------+--------------------+------------------+-------
Jane       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Jane       | Doe       | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
John       | Doe       | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99
John       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Bill       | Gates     | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99
Bill       | Gates     | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Bill       | Gates     | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99

Now, Jane Doe shows up before John Doe.

Combining the where and order by clauses

You can combine the where and order by clauses. The where clause must always come before the order by clause. Here is an example of using both clauses: 


bookstore_db=> select * from sales_view where last_name='Doe' order by price;
first_name | last_name |       title        |      author      | price 
-----------+-----------+--------------------+------------------+-------
Jane       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
John       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Jane       | Doe       | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
John       | Doe       | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99

Here all the sales that involve the last name of 'Doe' are shown sorted by the price field in ascending order. If you wanted Jane Doe's purchases to show first, you could do the following: 


bookstore_db=> select * from sales_view where last_name='Doe' order by first_name,price;
first_name | last_name |       title        |      author      | price 
-----------+-----------+--------------------+------------------+-------
Jane       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
Jane       | Doe       | The Cat in the Hat | Dr. Seuss        | 8.99
John       | Doe       | Go Dog Go          | P.D. Eastman     | 7.99
John       | Doe       | Ender's Game       | Orson Scott Card | 9.99
